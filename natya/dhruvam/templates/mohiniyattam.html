Introduction

Mohiniyattam, a classical dance from Kerala, is celebrated for its graceful movements and feminine expressions. It is closely linked to the myth of Mohini, the enchantress avatar of Vishnu. The name "Mohiniyattam" derives from "Mohini," the enchantress avatar of the Hindu deity Vishnu, and "attam," meaning dance. This dance form traditionally depicts feminine grace and charm and is often performed by women.

History

Originating from Kerala, Mohiniyattam has a deep-rooted history that dates back several centuries. This dance form was traditionally performed in temples and royal courts, often in praise of the deities. The delicate and flowing style of the dance was influenced by both the folk and classical traditions of Kerala.Over time, Mohiniyattam evolved, incorporating both Bharatanatyam and local dance traditions to create a distinct style. In the early 20th century, the dance was revived and modernized, gaining recognition as a classical dance form of India. It was revived and formalized in the 20th century by the renowned dancer and scholar, Vallathol Narayana Menon, who sought to restore and preserve this art form. The dance form combines elements of classical Indian dance with influences from regional folk traditions, creating a unique and captivating style. The performance of Mohiniyattam typically involves a solo dancer, who expresses themes of devotion, love, and mythology through a combination of intricate footwork, expressive hand gestures (mudras), and emotive facial expressions. The dance is accompanied by traditional Carnatic music, with a focus on vocal rendition and rhythmic patterns. Today, Mohiniyattam is celebrated for its aesthetic beauty and continues to be performed both in traditional settings and on global stages.

Style and Features

Mohiniyattam is characterized by its slow and graceful movements, with a strong emphasis on Lasya, the feminine aspect of dance. The dance is accompanied by soulful music and features hand gestures (mudras) and facial expressions that convey deep emotions. The dance form employs a combination of intricate footwork and expressive hand gestures, known as mudras, to narrate stories and evoke emotions. The movements are performed in a semi-sitting posture called "adavu," which allows for the graceful swaying of the hips and the subtle articulation of the torso. The emphasis is on maintaining a serene and composed demeanor, with movements that flow seamlessly from one to the next. The expressive aspect of Mohiniyattam is enhanced by its use of facial expressions (abhinaya), which are crucial in conveying the narrative and emotional content of the performance. The dance often depicts themes from Hindu mythology, with a focus on tales of divine love and devotion. Accompanied by traditional Carnatic music, Mohiniyattam features a rhythmic accompaniment that complements the dancer's movements. The music typically includes vocal renditions, melodic compositions, and intricate rhythmic patterns that align with the dance's nuanced expressions. Overall, Mohiniyattam's style and features reflect its deep roots in devotional practices, its emphasis on grace and fluidity, and its rich tradition of storytelling through dance.

Famous Practitioners

Prominent practitioners of Mohiniyattam include Sunanda Nair, Kalamandalam Kalyanikutty Amma, and Bharati Shivaji, who have all contributed immensely to the growth and recognition of Mohiniyattam as a classical dance form.
